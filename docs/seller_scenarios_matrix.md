# Seller Archetypes × Lifecycle — Effect Matrix (living design doc)

A working scratchpad for the seller-scenario brainstorm (owner item #5). The big idea (owner): a
seller's **type** sets hidden variables that ripple across **multiple steps** with cause-and-effect —
e.g. a tired syndicator almost certainly has deferred maintenance, but whether the **buyer finds it**
depends on how hard they run DD, and how the **renegotiation** goes depends on whether the seller has
partners. This doc holds: (1) the archetype roster + traits, (2) what each will/won't move on in the
LOI, (3) effects per step, (4) the cross-step mechanics, (5) the response-time model, (6) open gaps.

Legend: `H/M/L` = high/med/low · `❓` = needs owner input · *(new)* = not yet in the game.

## 4. Cross-step cause-&-effect mechanics (the important part)

### 4a. Deferred-maintenance discovery (DM × DD intensity)
A hidden **DM level** is set by seller type (col in §1). Whether the buyer **finds** it — and the
time/cost to do so — depends on the **DD path** they choose:

| Buyer DD choice | Cost | Time | Discovery of hidden DM | Formal report? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 quick walkthrough (self) | ~free | fast | Low — misses most | No |
| 3+ visits (self + volunteers) | ~free (favors) | slower | Med-High | No |
| Hire 3rd-party DD firm | $$ | slower | High — finds it | **Yes (formal report)** |
| 3rd-party + 3+ visits | $$$ | slowest | Highest | Yes |

- Finding DM → unlocks a **price/credit renegotiation** (a new mini-negotiation).
- The renegotiation's success depends on the seller's **decision structure** (§4b).
- Teaching point: cheap/lazy DD saves time+money now but eats returns later in AM (the surprise capex).

### 4b. Decision structure → negotiation behavior
| Structure | Reply speed | Re-trade risk | Can a single party kill it? | Renegotiation after DM found |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Solo | Fast | Low | No | Direct, quick |
| GP + LP partners (syndicator) | Med | Med | LP vote can stall | Needs partner sign-off → slow |
| Heirs (multiple) | Slow | High | Yes (one balks) | Emotional, uneven |
| Institutional committee | Slow | Low | Process-bound | Rare; firm |
| Two disputing partners | Erratic | High | **Yes** | Whipsaws |

### 4c. Tone-matching (owner's institutional point)
Some sellers force the **buyer's** conversational style. Institutional = dry, binary, no small talk;
if the buyer tries to "build rapport" or leaves wiggle room, it reads as weakness / wastes their time.
→ Model a **tone-match** check: pick the response register that matches the seller, or lose ground.
